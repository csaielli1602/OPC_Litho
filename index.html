About this tool

This tool is about photolithography — printing a chip’s features by shining light through a stencil called a photomask. At modern feature sizes the shapes are smaller than the wavelength of the light drawing them, so what lands on the wafer is a blurred version of what’s on the mask.

Chipmakers fight back by drawing the mask “wrong” on purpose: optical proximity correction (OPC) adds nudges, serifs, and hammerheads so the blur lands on the shape they actually wanted, while shaped illumination and phase-shift masks squeeze out extra sharpness. Every layer of every chip depends on these corrections being right.

Everything here is a simplified teaching version, not a real optical simulation. No diffraction is calculated — the printed shapes are hand-tuned to move in the right direction when you flip a switch. Production OPC runs rigorous, computationally intensive imaging models on large compute clusters for every mask, accurate to nanometers.

If the puzzle of “draw the wrong shape to print the right one” appeals to you, it’s a genuine full-time discipline in the fab — lithography and OPC engineers do exactly this for a living.

Concepts follow standard semiconductor lithography references (optical proximity correction, off-axis illumination, sub-resolution assist features, alternating phase-shift masks). Companion piece to the Czochralski crystal-pulling simulator.
